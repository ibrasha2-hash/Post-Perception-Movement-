# 11. The Artist's Practical Guide: A Methodology for Creating Post-Perception Artworks

## Ten Steps from Concept to Completion

**Ibrahim Shalabi**

---

## Abstract

This practical guide provides step-by-step methodology for artists creating Post-Perception works. Drawing on the movement's foundational principles, the guide offers ten actionable steps (from sensory disruption to open endings), illustrated with examples from the movement and accompanied by practical exercises. Each step is designed to ensure the artwork functions as a perceptual test rather than merely a shocking experience.

---

## The Ten-Step Methodology

### Step 1: Begin with the Senses, Not the Idea
Start by asking: "What sensory disruption do I want to create?" Not: "What idea do I want to express?"

### Step 2: Deconstruct the Familiar
Choose highly familiar objects (pipes, urinals, household appliances) and reframe them in ways that destabilize certainty.

### Step 3: Build Perceptual Paradoxes
Create sensory contradictions using one of five types:
- Visual-linguistic
- Visual-auditory
- Visual-tactile
- Functional-contextual
- Temporal-existential

### Step 4: Choose Medium Deliberately
Select medium based on the specific disruption needed, not based on novelty or fashion.

### Step 5: Add Text If Needed
Use text (if at all) as contradiction, not explanation. The work should communicate through sensory experience.

### Step 6: Design a Role for the Viewer
The viewer must have a role: physical participant, decision-maker, contributor, witness, or existential tester.

### Step 7: Use Time as a Tool
Time is malleable: slowing, accelerating, repeating, fading, suspending, looping—all are techniques.

### Step 8: Test on Yourself First
Before showing the work to others, be the first test subject. Record your experience; assess if genuine disruption occurred.

### Step 9: Document Others' Experiences
Viewer responses are part of the work. Document through interviews, questionnaires, video, follow-up contact.

### Step 10: Leave the Work Open
Avoid didacticism. Leave questions unanswered. Resist wall texts that explain. Let the work remain ambiguous.

---

## Evaluation Criteria

Use these five questions to evaluate your own work:

1. **Perceptual disruption:** Did the work cause genuine disruption?
2. **Cognitive engagement:** Did it provoke thinking, or merely shock?
3. **Viewer participation:** Did the viewer have a genuine role?
4. **Temporal persistence:** Did the effect linger after the experience?
5. **Philosophical depth:** Can the work be read on multiple levels?

**If you answer "yes" to at least three: your work deserves attention.**

---

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Solution |
|---------|----------|
| Shock without depth | Test for lingering effects |
| Technological spectacle | Ask: does tech serve the idea? |
| Over-explanation | Remove wall texts |
| Passive viewer | Design a role |
| Cultural insensitivity | Test with diverse audiences |

---

## Conclusion

Creating a Post-Perception artwork requires shifting from object-maker to test-designer. Your role is not to create beauty but to design perceptual experiences that train the viewer to resist deception.
